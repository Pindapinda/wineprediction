The birth of quantitative wine analysis

In 1993 Orley Ashenfelter, professor in Economics at Princeton University, shocked wine connoisseurs, investors and oenologists1 alike, by presenting announcing that he could predict the quality of a wine several years before it is ready to drink. Ashenfelter wanted a reliably way to find good ones that fit in his budget, which required that he outperform the current system: Traditionally wine tasters would wait until four months after the wine has been placed in a barrel and then start to taste the mixture, long before it has developed it's final taste. Using their experience wine tasters would then try to predict whether the wine would ultimately taste well. This has proven to be a difficult process, as confirmed by Bruce Kaiser, the former director of wine auctioneering at Butterfield & Butterfield who said: "Young wines are changing so quickly that no one, and I mean no one, can really evaluate a vintage at all accurately by taste until it is at least ten years old, perhaps older." So when Ashenfelter announced that he could do what professional tasters couldn't based solely on some weather information something something something.

The formula

In his paper which he republished in 2008 Ashenfelter investigates five factors that he thinks could affect Bordeaux wine prices. Because young Bordeaux wines are more astringent many people prefer to drink these wines after they have aged a bit, meaning that wines are traded when they are young and sold for more once they are older. In the end Ashenfelter settled on using three weather-related variables, in addition to the age of the wine, which allows him to explain approximately 83% of all variation in the average price of Bordeaux wine vintages. Below we show exactly what these variables are and how they matter.
